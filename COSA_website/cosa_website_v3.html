the stairs where made following the natural landscape considering wate flow directions. the borders are make out of sidewlak blocks that are filled with gravel. the zig-zagged stairs allow planting in the created niches. Erosion grass and other local species where planted along the sides.

We chose to use a local method for the fence line—the branches of three varieties of propagable trees are used as cuttings (“madero-negro”). They are planted during full/empty moon around the month of May, in order to ensure success in growing into a lovely, dense, green fence.

These are some of the favorite plants and trees from the property. There are two massive guanacaste (the national Costa Rican tree), a corteza amarilla that blossoms with bright yellow flowers for three days around March, and roble sabana, an oakish tree that blossoms in pink. These are hardwoods and great for furniture making. Otherwise we planted fruit trees like mango, avocado, and lemon varieties. Another interesting plant is the cordyline (hoja-roja), locally used to mark borders due to its distinct pink. We planted and maintained many other local plants that attract butterflies and bees.

In the terraced land we used a structure of plants that support the soil. The coconut trees are planted on the outline of each flat area each 10m; the “vetiver” is a high resistance erosion grass that holds the soil, and it is planted in a zig-zag, also called “pata-gallina” (chicken foot).

ABOUT
This project is an exploration making through living. I designed the space, built all the featured furniture, from the kitchen and doors down to the towel hangers. Most of the furniture is made out of leftover construction materials. this website was also designed by myself in order to present the project.

The project came out of a need to explore the term "home." It started after traveling and eventually living in Costa Rica, which led to finding a property and developing the project from there. Over time, it became a way for me to learn the different sectors involved in designing a house, as well as my own position within them and within a larger system. Planning and sketching the house, approving the architectural plans in the municipality, working on the foundations with constructors, and eventually taking on most of the hands-on work to the extent of my ability. Around 90% of the materials used in the furniture objects were sourced from leftover construction materials, which shaped many of the decisions and details along the way.

During the time I lived in the house, it functioned as a working studio and as a home in the process of becoming.

I began this project as a personal search. While traveling in Central and South America, I was contemplating what “home” is, where I feel at home, and where I can have a sense of belonging. These questions continue to accompany me, and I believe the idea of home grows with a person's life. Living in Costa Rica and doing this project became part of that process, deepening my understanding of how different ideas and realities shape building a home. Trials, mistakes, and a long series of decisions shaped the final result.

Being as involved as possible in the house’s design was important to me. I began with hand-drawn sketches, which evolved into architectural plans with a local architect. A contractor built the structural shell, then I managed the project with subcontractors. When the house was somewhat livable, I moved in and created from within. I did most of the interior work and furniture by hand. The house was built conventionally, but most furniture was made from leftover materials, which shaped both the design and the final furniture forms.

living room

a large corridor leads you to the living room. It is the central space of the house and it was designed as an open area that allows for comfort and a connection to the outdoors. Above the glass vitrines, a pair of mashrabiyas enable continuous airflow through the corridor. The upper openings release hot air, and constant airflow maintains the space cool without the need of air-conditioning.

kitchen

The kitchen was the first element I built. It is connected to the living room, allowing the act of cooking to become social. It offers a variety of storage solutions and is highly functional.

bathroom

The bathroom is designed to connect with nature while maintaining privacy. An opening to the terrace allows constant ventilation, and a frosted window enables either enclosure or openness to the outside. The shower head directs water onto a curved metal surface supported by a concrete structure, allowing the water to fall freely. The sink is cast in concrete and incorporates a floating shelf made from leftover materials-wood and copper plumbing pipes.

bedroom

The sleeping areas are designed to allow privacy and function almost as autonomous units. The bedroom connects directly to the bathroom, while the glass facade opens to a covered private terrace. A passage between the terrace and the bathroom creates a circular flow within the room. The space forms an intimate alcove, in contrast to the open nature of the living area. Furniture and lighting are minimal, supporting rest and sleep.

detail

Little house features were made out of a combination of materials, from scrap wood to hand made ceramic hangers and objects from the local flea market.
